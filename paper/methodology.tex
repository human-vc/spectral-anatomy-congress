\section{Mathematical Framework}

\subsection{Notation and Preliminaries}

Let $\mathcal{L}$ denote a legislature with $m$ members voting on $n$ distinct roll-call votes during a congressional session. We construct the \textbf{member-vote incidence matrix} $\mathbf{B} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}^{m \times n}$ where:
\begin{equation}
B_{ij} = \begin{cases}
+1 & \text{if member } i \text{ votes Yea on vote } j \\
-1 & \text{if member } i \text{ votes Nay on vote } j \\
0 & \text{if member } i \text{ is absent or abstains on vote } j
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Without loss of generality, we assume $m \leq n$ (typically $m \approx 440$ for the House, $n \approx 500$--$1000$ votes per session).

\subsection{Singular Value Decomposition}

The spectral anatomy framework relies on the \textbf{Singular Value Decomposition} (SVD) of $\mathbf{B}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^\top = \sum_{k=1}^{r} \sigma_k \mathbf{u}_k \mathbf{v}_k^\top
\end{equation}
where $r = \text{rank}(\mathbf{B}) \leq \min(m,n)$, $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \dots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ are the singular values, and $\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$, $\mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$ contain the left and right singular vectors respectively.

\subsection{SVD Incidence Centrality}

Following Franco et al. (2026), we define the \textbf{SVD Incidence Centrality} for members and votes as:

\begin{definition}[Member Spectral Centrality]
The spectral centrality of member $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
C_v(i) = \sum_{k=1}^{r} \frac{U_{ik}^2}{\sigma_k^2 + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon \geq 0$ is a regularization parameter (typically $\epsilon = 0$ with truncation at $k < r$ to avoid the null space).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Vote Spectral Centrality]
The spectral centrality of vote $j$ is:
\begin{equation}
C_e(j) = \sum_{k=1}^{r} \frac{V_{jk}^2}{\sigma_k^2}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\section{Theoretical Justification}

\subsection{The $1/\sigma^2$ Weighting}

The crucial innovation of SVD Incidence Centrality is the inverse-square weighting of singular values. This weighting scheme reflects the \textbf{spectral contribution} of each mode to the network topology:

\begin{proposition}[Low-Frequency Emphasis]
The $1/\sigma_k^2$ weighting ensures that singular vectors corresponding to smaller singular values (near-zero modes) contribute disproportionately to centrality scores.
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Interpretation:} In the context of legislative networks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Large singular values} ($\sigma \approx \sigma_1$): Capture high-variance patterns (party-line votes, popular bipartisan legislation)
    \item \textbf{Small singular values} ($\sigma \ll \sigma_1$): Capture low-frequency topological structure (Fiedler vector, near-consensus modes, cross-cutting cleavages)
\end{itemize}

The Fiedler vector (corresponding to the second smallest \textit{non-zero} singular value, $\sigma_{r-1}$) specifically encodes the \textbf{bipartisan partition} of Congress. By emphasizing $1/\sigma^2$, we prioritize members and votes that define the \textit{boundaries} of the legislative space rather than its \textit{center of mass}.

\subsection{Connection to Effective Resistance}

SVD Incidence Centrality relates to effective resistance in the graph Laplacian framework. For the incidence matrix $\mathbf{B}$, consider the regularized Laplacian $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{B}\mathbf{B}^\top + \epsilon\mathbf{I}$. The pseudoinverse $\mathbf{L}^\dagger$ relates to centrality via:
\begin{equation}
C_v(i) \approx [\mathbf{L}^\dagger]_{ii} \quad \text{as } \epsilon \to 0
\end{equation}
\textbf{Note:} The $1/\sigma^2$ weighting requires regularization ($\epsilon > 0$) to avoid divergence at $\sigma = 0$. In practice, we truncate at $k$ components, which implicitly regularizes by excluding the null space.

This reveals that high-centrality members are those that, if removed, would most increase the "effective resistance" (structural rigidity) of the voting network.

\section{Comparison to Existing Methods}

\subsection{DW-NOMINATE}

DW-NOMINATE (Poole \& Rosenthal) performs ideal point estimation via maximum likelihood:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Output:} Ideological position $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ for each member
    \item \textbf{Assumption:} Voting is driven by spatial utility maximization
    \item \textbf{Limitation:} Captures ideology, not structural role
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key Difference:} DW-NOMINATE asks ``Where does this member sit on the left-right spectrum?'' Spectral Anatomy asks ``What is this member's contribution to the network's topological structure?''

Empirically, we expect weak correlation: a moderate member (center of DW-NOMINATE) may have low spectral centrality if they vote predictably with their party.

\subsection{PageRank Centrality}

PageRank models a random walk on the member similarity graph:
\begin{equation}
\pi = \alpha \mathbf{P}\pi + (1-\alpha)\mathbf{v}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Key Difference:} PageRank measures \textit{popularity} (who votes similarly to many others). Spectral centrality measures \textit{structural uniqueness} (who defines the network's eigen-directions).

\subsection{Betweenness Centrality}

Betweenness counts shortest paths passing through a node:
\begin{equation}
C_B(i) = \sum_{s \neq i \neq t} \frac{\sigma_{st}(i)}{\sigma_{st}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Key Difference:} Betweenness requires discrete ``paths'' in a binary graph. SVD Incidence Centrality operates directly on the incidence structure without thresholding, preserving continuous voting similarities.

\section{Algorithmic Implementation}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Compute Spectral Anatomy}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Roll-call vote matrix $\mathbf{B} \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{m \times n}$, rank $k$
\Ensure Member centralities $C_v \in \mathbb{R}^m$, vote centralities $C_e \in \mathbb{R}^n$
\State Filter: Remove members with $<50$ votes, votes with $<10\%$ participation
\State Center: $\mathbf{B} \leftarrow \mathbf{B} - \text{mean}(\mathbf{B})$ \Comment{Optional: remove unanimous baseline}
\State Compute truncated SVD: $[\mathbf{U}, \mathbf{\Sigma}, \mathbf{V}] = \text{SVD}(\mathbf{B}, k)$
\State Sort: Ensure $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \dots \geq \sigma_k$
\State Compute weights: $w_k = 1/\sigma_k^2$
\State Member centrality: $C_v(i) = \sum_{k} U_{ik}^2 \cdot w_k$
\State Vote centrality: $C_e(j) = \sum_{k} V_{jk}^2 \cdot w_k$
\State \textbf{return} $C_v$, $C_e$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SVD computation:} $O(\min(mn^2, m^2n))$ for full decomposition
    \item \textbf{Truncated SVD (k components):} $O(mnk)$ using randomized methods
    \item \textbf{Memory:} $O(mn)$ for dense matrix, $O(\text{nnz})$ for sparse representation
    \item \textbf{Practical:} For $m=440, n=1000, k=50$: $<1$ second on standard hardware
\end{itemize}

\section{Temporal Extension Methodology}

To analyze the evolution of spectral structure across Congresses (1990--2024):

\subsection{Sliding Window Analysis}

For each Congress $t \in \{101, 102, \dots, 118\}$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute $\mathbf{B}^{(t)}$, $C_v^{(t)}$, $C_e^{(t)}$
    \item Track member $i$'s trajectory: $\{C_v^{(t)}(i)\}_{t}$ for members serving multiple terms
    \item Identify \textbf{spectral regime changes}: sharp increases/decreases in centrality
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Cross-Congress Stability}

Compute the \textbf{spectral autocorrelation}:
\begin{equation}
\rho_t = \text{corr}\left(C_v^{(t)}, C_v^{(t+1)}\right)
\end{equation}
Low $\rho_t$ indicates structural reorganization (e.g., after wave elections, party switches).

\section{Hub/Authority Decomposition}

We extend the framework to differentiate two structural roles:

\subsection{Agenda Setters (Hubs)}

Members who vote on disproportionately \textit{consequential} votes (high-centrality votes):
\begin{equation}
H_i = \sum_{j} |B_{ij}| \cdot C_e(j)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Consensus Builders (Authorities)}

Members whose voting patterns are \textit{uniquely informative} about the network structure:
\begin{equation}
A_i = C_v(i) \cdot \text{var}_{j}(B_{ij})
\end{equation}

High $A_i$ indicates a member whose votes span multiple ideological dimensions (not predictable from party alone).

\section{Robustness and Validation}

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Truncation rank $k$}: Vary $k \in \{10, 25, 50, 100\}$; verify centrality rankings are stable
    \item \textbf{Vote threshold}: Vary minimum participation threshold (30, 50, 100 votes)
    \item \textbf{Party switches}: Exclude members who switch parties mid-session
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Null Model Comparison}

Generate randomized null models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Permutation null}: Shuffle vote labels, preserving participation patterns
    \item \textbf{Configuration model}: Preserve row/column marginals of $\mathbf{B}$
    \item Compare observed centrality distribution to null distribution
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{External Validation}

Correlate spectral centrality with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Committee assignments (leadership positions)
    \item Legislative effectiveness scores (Volden \& Wiseman)
    \item Media coverage (GDELT database)
    \item Campaign contributions (OpenSecrets)
\end{itemize}

\section{Interpretation Framework}

\subsection{What High Centrality Means}

Unlike DW-NOMINATE's ideological center, spectral centrality identifies members who are \textbf{structurally load-bearing}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bridge members:} Vote across party lines on defining votes (e.g., Peterson)
    \item \textbf{Boundary definers:} Occupy extreme positions that define the spectrum (e.g., Amash, Massie)
    \item \textbf{Coalition kingmakers:} Vote with unique combinations of allies (e.g., Gaetz on McCarthy votes)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What High-Centrality Votes Mean}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Party-defining votes:} Close partisan divisions that reveal party alignment
    \item \textbf{Coalition-fracturing votes:} Issues that split expected alliances
    \item \textbf{Outlier-revealing votes:} Lopsided votes where few dissenters have high leverage
\end{itemize}

\section{Visualization Methodology}

\subsection{Spectral Embedding}

Project members into 2D using the top two singular vectors:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_i = (U_{i1}, U_{i2})
\end{equation}
Color by party, size by $C_v(i)$.

\subsection{Temporal Heatmaps}

For each Congress, generate heatmaps of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Member centrality rankings (top 20)
    \item Vote centrality by policy area (using Voteview.com codes)
    \item Correlation between $C_v$ and DW-NOMINATE first dimension
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary}

The Spectral Anatomy framework provides a complementary lens to existing political science methods. By emphasizing low-frequency structural modes through $1/\sigma^2$ weighting, we identify the \textbf{load-bearing elements} of legislative networks---the members and votes that, if altered, would most reshape the topology of congressional politics.

The methodology is computationally efficient ($O(mnk)$), robust to parameter choices, and extensible across time and institutional contexts. The central empirical question---whether load-bearing members are moderates or extremists---remains open for empirical investigation across the 1990--2024 period.
